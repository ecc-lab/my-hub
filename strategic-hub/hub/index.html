The 4 Mindset Shifts

Your tools, experience, and network are already there. The bottleneck is internal. These are the four specific rewirings that unlock Game B — the executive market player's game. Click each to expand.

The core diagnosis: You have been rewarded for being excellent inside a structure. The next phase rewards you for being excellent as the structure — as the authority, the brand, the asset that companies and PE firms seek out.

Shift 01
Employee → Asset
What does Orona need? → What is my market value?

Your skills and experience have market value independent of any employer. Orona doesn't own your capabilities — they rent them. The first shift is starting to think like the owner of an asset, not the holder of a job.

This isn't disloyalty to Orona. It's professional maturity. Every board member, PE partner, and independent director thinks this way.

Exercise Write down 5 capabilities you have that are not specific to Orona. Frame them as outcomes: "I can take a €0 country operation to €Xm in Y years." These are your assets. Name them.
Shift 02
Permission → Initiative
Wait to be selected → Engineer your selection

The employee brain waits. The market player acts. You don't wait for a headhunter to call about a board seat. You systematically build relationships with the people who create board seats — PE operating partners, search firm partners, YPO peers who chair boards.

This feels like self-promotion. It's not. It's how every independent director in Europe operates.

Exercise Identify 3 people in your network right now who are connected to board opportunities. What's stopping you from having an explicit conversation with them today? Write the exact message you'd send. The discomfort you feel writing it IS the employee mindset.
Shift 03
No Time → Strategic Time
I'll do this when I have time → 2hrs/week, non-negotiable

You will never have more time. Two kids will become three activities each. Work will always have an urgent thing. The people who build board careers alongside demanding roles don't have more time — they treat strategic development as sacred.

2 hours per week = 1 meaningful conversation + 1 LinkedIn post + 1 YPO follow-up. That's a board career being built in the background.

Exercise Block 2 hours in your calendar this week. Label it "Strategic Development." Don't cancel it for anything except a genuine emergency. Do this for 4 weeks before evaluating if it works.
Shift 04
Monetize → Become Known
How do I make money on the side? → How do I become the obvious choice?

Monetization is downstream of reputation, not a prerequisite for it. The question isn't "how do I earn extra income?" — it's "how do I become the most recognized industrial operations executive in CEE and Spain?" When you achieve that, the income follows automatically.

Your AI interest is a credential, not a side hustle. An MD who understands AI applications in manufacturing is extremely rare and extremely valuable as a board member right now.

Exercise Write one sentence describing who you are in the market — not your job title. It should answer: Who do I help? What outcome do I create? Why me? That sentence is your positioning statement.

The YPO Playbook

YPO is not a networking club — it's a peer-to-peer trust network of 38,000 CEOs. Used correctly, it's your highest-leverage asset for landing a paid board seat. Most members never use it this strategically.

The YPO paradox: Stop asking "what can I get from this network?" and start asking "who do I genuinely want to know, and what can I offer?" The second question generates far more for you. Trust is the currency. YPO works on peer-to-peer referral — one member's recommendation carries 100x more weight than a cold LinkedIn message to a PE firm.

What board membership actually is

Myth
Reality
"Board members give strategic advice and high-level guidance."

"It's a few hours a month — easy second income."
PE-backed industrial NED = 20–30 days per year. Each board meeting requires 100–200 pages of pre-reading. Committee work doubles the load. Crisis events can demand weeks unexpectedly.

You actively keep your operational instincts in check. The rule is "nose in, fingers out." If you start managing, you stop governing — and lose the chair's trust.

Good NEDs talk 20% of the time. Their value is in the questions they ask, not the answers they give. The best question gets asked once and changes the direction of the discussion.

"Board members work for the company."
Board members work for the shareholders. In PE, the fund expects you to defend their interests when they conflict with management. This is uncomfortable. It is the job.

⚠️ Poland-Specific Liability
In Poland, the parent company can issue "binding instructions" to subsidiaries under the holding law (added 2022). D&O policies cover legal defense costs and settlements for negligence — they do NOT cover intentional fraud, criminal acts, or in some cases claims by the company itself. ⚠️ Insolvency Shifts Duties

If you sense financial distress: (1) demand independent advice immediately; (2) document every meeting; (3) consider resignation if board fails to act on creditor protection.

Five foundational topics every NED must internalise before stepping into a boardroom. Expand each to study in depth.

Boards govern, they don't manage. As a NED you provide oversight, challenge, and strategic counsel — but you are not part of the executive team. The distinction sounds obvious; in practice it's the hardest thing to internalise for someone coming from a COO/MD role.

Typical Annual Board Cycle
Key Board Committees
Audit Committee
Financial integrity, internal controls, external auditor relationship. Typically 3 INEDs. Chair often a qualified accountant.
Remuneration Committee
Executive pay, incentive design, benchmarking. Politically sensitive. INEDs only. Reports to shareholders at AGM.
Nomination Committee
Board composition, succession planning, diversity targets. Often chaired by the Board Chair. Your entry point.
The pre-read principle: Effective NEDs read board packs 72h before the meeting, form a view, and arrive with 2–3 precise questions. You're not there to wing it. Quality of challenge, not quantity of words.

PE-backed boards operate under fundamentally different logic than listed or family businesses. The fund has a finite horizon — typically 5–7 years — and every governance decision is filtered through value creation and exit readiness.

Fund Economics
The GP earns carry on returns above an 8% hurdle. Every year of delay costs ~1.2–1.5x multiple erosion. This shapes urgency in a way listed boards don't feel.
100-Day Playbook
First 3 months post-close are disproportionately important. Board is expected to validate or challenge the thesis, not just observe. Prepare a view on Day 1.
Exit Orientation
Every strategic decision is framed as: does this increase enterprise value at exit? Trade sale vs IPO vs secondary buyout — NEDs must understand the preferred route.
Leverage & Covenants
Most PE-backed businesses carry debt at 4–7x EBITDA. NEDs must understand covenant structures and the consequences of a breach.
Management Package
Management holds sweet equity (typically 5–15% of upside). This aligns incentives but creates tensions — management may resist board challenge if it delays exit.
Your edge in PE boards: You've integrated 30+ acquisitions and run a PE-adjacent manufacturing P&L. You understand hold period logic, integration risk, and the difference between reporting and reality. That's rare at NED level.

The Chair is the single most important relationship for a NED. They control agenda-setting, board dynamics, your reappointment, and your access to the CEO. Getting this relationship right is non-negotiable.

Do
  • Seek a 1:1 before the first board meeting
  • Ask: "What would make you feel this board is working?"
  • Raise concerns directly with the Chair before the meeting
  • Support their agenda in public; push back in private
  • Build rapport over time — they recommended you
Don't
  • Surprise them with a boardroom ambush
  • Form caucuses with other NEDs against the Chair
  • Go directly to the CEO without the Chair's knowledge
  • Push back on every item — pick your battles
  • Assume your operator experience trumps their process
Senior Independent Director (SID): In UK-governed boards the SID is a sounding board for the Chair and an escalation point for shareholders. If the Chair is also the founder, the SID becomes especially important. Know who the SID is before you join.

Boardrooms are high-stakes social environments where ambiguity is the default. These six scenarios cover the situations that catch first-time NEDs off-guard.

01 · The CEO Overpromises
Situation: CEO presents a 40% revenue growth target that doesn't match the data. Response: Pre-meeting: email the CFO asking for model assumptions. In meeting: "Can you walk us through the key drivers behind the 40%?" — forces the number into the open without confrontation.
02 · Conflict of Interest (Yours)
Situation: An acquisition target is a company where you know the founder. Response: Declare it immediately to the Chair — before the meeting, in writing. Offer to recuse. Doing this proactively builds enormous trust. Never manage a conflict by staying quiet.
03 · CEO Requests Personal Favour
Situation: CEO asks you to make an introduction for their personal venture. Response: Politely decline until your term ends. You are the company's NED, not the CEO's personal advisor. Blurring this line creates liability and dependency.
04 · Majority Shareholder Pressure
Situation: PE sponsor calls you directly to lobby for a specific vote outcome. Response: Log the call. Inform the Chair. Vote based on independent judgment. If the pressure persists and compromises your independence, resign rather than comply.
05 · Financial Irregularity Suspicion
Situation: A whistleblower email alleges expense fraud by the CFO. Response: Do not forward internally. Contact the Audit Committee Chair and your personal lawyer within 24h. You now have constructive knowledge — doing nothing is a breach of duty.
06 · Board Pack Never Arrives on Time
Situation: Board packs consistently arrive 18h before the meeting. Response: Raise it with the Chair after your first meeting — frame it as enabling the board to add value. If it persists, formalise a 72h delivery policy in the ToR. This is a governance quality signal.

Pain + Reflection = Progress

Dalio's most underrated principle. Most people experience discomfort and either push through blindly or retreat. The winners stop and examine the pain — because it always contains information about what's not working in the machine.

Not all advice is equal. Dalio weights opinions by the credibility of the source — specifically, people who have successfully done the thing you're trying to do, repeatedly, and can explain why.

Radical Transparency + The Two Selves

The human brain is wired to register negative events 3–5 times more powerfully than positive ones (Baumeister et al., 2001). This was evolutionarily useful — missing the berry bush is fine; missing the predator is fatal. But it means your brain systematically overweights failures, embarrassments, and evidence of inadequacy, while rapidly discounting and forgetting achievements and successes. This is not a personality flaw — it is hardware.

Neuroplasticity — The Good News
The brain is not fixed. Every thought you think repeatedly strengthens the neural pathway for that thought — making it easier and faster to think it next time. Cognitive Distortion Identification CBT — Burns, 1980
There are 10 classic cognitive distortions — systematic errors in thinking that feel true but are objectively demonstrable as false. Impostor syndrome typically uses four of them in combination.
The four distortions driving your impostor pattern
1
Discounting positives: "The Orona success doesn't count because the company gave me the structure." Counter: did the structure run itself, or did you build the team, culture, and results?
2
Comparison fallacy: Comparing your internal experience to others' external presentation. Counter: you have zero data on their internal experience.
3
Emotional reasoning: "I feel like a fraud, therefore I am one." Counter: feelings are not evidence. The court doesn't convict on feelings.
4
Mind reading: "They can tell I'm not as good as they thought." Counter: you have no evidence of what they're thinking. This is projection, not perception.
Cognitive Defusion ACT — Hayes, 2004
Acceptance and Commitment Therapy's most powerful tool for impostor syndrome. The insight: you don't need to believe your thoughts, argue with them, or eliminate them. You just need to stop fusing with them — stop treating them as reality rather than as events in your mind. The thought "I don't belong here" is not you. It is a thought your mind produced. There is a difference.
The defusion technique
1
When the impostor thought appears, instead of "I don't belong here" — say internally: "I notice my mind is having the thought that I don't belong here."
2
Then: "Thank you, mind. I see you're trying to protect me." This is not sarcasm — it is genuine acknowledgment that the amygdala is doing its job.
3
Then: "And I'm going to walk into this room and be present anyway." You don't argue with the thought. You defuse it and act from your values despite it.
4
Over weeks of practice, the thought loses its emotional charge. It becomes background noise rather than a stop signal.
Values Clarification + Committed Action ACT — Hayes, 2004
ACT argues that the goal is not to eliminate negative emotion — it is to act in alignment with your values regardless of what your emotions are doing. You don't wait until the impostor feeling goes away to have the YPO conversation. You have the YPO conversation in the presence of the feeling, because your values — growth, contribution, connection — matter more than your comfort.
The values-action matrix

2
For each action you're avoiding (YPO conversation, board application, LinkedIn post): ask "Is avoiding this action consistent with my values?"
3
If no: commit to the action not because the fear is gone, but because your values require it.
4
The feeling of fear is allowed to come with you. It just doesn't get to drive.
The Inner Critic as a Protector Internal Family Systems — Schwartz, 1995
IFS (Internal Family Systems) offers the most sophisticated model for working with self-criticism. The key insight: the inner critic is not your enemy. It is a protective part of your psyche that developed when you were young, in an environment where being "too much" or "not enough" had real social consequences. It learned to pre-emptively criticize you to prevent others from doing it first. It is trying to protect you. It is just using a strategy that no longer serves you.
The IFS approach — talk to the critic, don't fight it
1
When the inner critic fires ("you don't belong in YPO"), instead of fighting it — acknowledge it. Internally: "I hear you. What are you afraid will happen if I claim my place in this room?"
2
Listen for the fear under the criticism. It is usually something like: "They'll reject you" or "You'll embarrass yourself" or "You'll lose what you have."
3
Respond to the fear, not the criticism: "I understand you're trying to protect me from rejection. I appreciate that. And I'm capable of handling whatever happens. I've handled harder things."
4
Ask the critic to step back — not to disappear. "Can you trust me to handle this?" Over time, the critic relaxes as it learns you can manage the challenges it fears.
Self-Compassion — The Three Components Kristin Neff, 2011
Dr. Kristin Neff's research established that self-compassion — not self-esteem — is the most reliable predictor of psychological resilience and sustained high performance. Self-esteem is conditional (I feel good when I succeed). Self-compassion is unconditional (I treat myself with the same care I'd give a trusted friend, regardless of outcome). High achievers almost universally have very high standards for others and punishing standards for themselves. This asymmetry is both irrational and damaging.
The three components — practice all three simultaneously
1
Mindfulness: Acknowledge the painful feeling without exaggerating or suppressing it. "This is a moment of self-doubt. It feels uncomfortable right now."
2
Common humanity: Recognize that self-doubt is universal, not a personal failing. "Everyone in that YPO room has felt exactly this. I am not uniquely broken."
3
Self-kindness: Speak to yourself as you would to a close friend who came to you with this exact doubt. What would you say to them? Say that to yourself.

Narrative Therapy — Reauthoring Your Story White & Epston, 1990
Narrative therapy argues that we don't live our lives — we live our stories about our lives. The impostor narrative is a story: "I got lucky. I don't fully deserve this. Fixed vs Growth Mindset Carol Dweck, 2006
